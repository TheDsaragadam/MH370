8 March 2014

The flight that vanished between routine and unknown

Malaysia Airlines Flight MH370 left Kuala Lumpur for Beijing with 239 people on board. Less than an hour later, routine radio contact ended. What followed became one of aviation's longest and most difficult searches.

By Dheeraj Saragadam

What is known

MH370 was a Boeing 777-200ER operating a scheduled passenger service from Kuala Lumpur International Airport to Beijing Capital International Airport. The aircraft disappeared from civilian radar after passing waypoint IGARI. Malaysian military radar and satellite data later shifted the search focus toward the southern Indian Ocean.

Timeline

A short timeline of the disappearance, the search, and later confirmed developments.

  1. 00:41, 8 Mar 2014

    Departure from Kuala Lumpur

    MH370 departs for Beijing with 227 passengers and 12 crew members.

  2. 01:19

    Last voice contact

    Air traffic control receives the final radio transmission from the cockpit as the aircraft approaches the handoff to Vietnamese airspace.

  3. 01:21

    Transponder contact is lost

    The aircraft is no longer visible to civilian secondary radar. Search-and-rescue efforts initially focus on the South China Sea.

  4. 24 Mar 2014

    Southern Indian Ocean becomes central

    Analysis of satellite communications leads Malaysian officials to state that the flight ended in the southern Indian Ocean.

  5. Jul 2015

    First confirmed debris

    A flaperon found on Réunion Island is later confirmed to be from MH370, giving physical evidence that the aircraft ended in the Indian Ocean.

  6. Jan 2017

    Official underwater search suspended

    The Australia-led search is suspended after scanning a large area of seabed without finding the main wreckage.

  7. 30 Jul 2018

    Malaysia releases final safety report

    The Malaysian ICAO Annex 13 Safety Investigation Team releases its report. It does not identify a definitive cause for the loss.

  8. 25 Mar 2025

    New Ocean Infinity agreement

    Malaysia formalises a renewed "no find, no fee" seabed search agreement with Ocean Infinity for a new area in the southern Indian Ocean.

  9. 8 Mar 2026

    Search update reports no confirmed find

    Malaysian authorities report that the renewed search has not produced confirmed evidence of the aircraft's location.

Why the search became so hard

01

Remote ocean

It might just be in the middle of the ocean or in a isolated island

02

Indirect data

Investigators had no flight recorder. Much of the search logic relied on radar traces, satellite handshakes, drift modelling, and probability which sometimes is inaccurate.

03

Ocean drift

Debris discoveries helped confirm the ocean region, but floating parts can travel for months across currents before reaching shore.

The unresolved questions

The timeline leaves major questions unanswered: why the aircraft turned away from its planned route, what happened on board, and exactly where the main wreckage rests. Until the flight recorders or major wreckage are recovered, any explanation remains incomplete.
